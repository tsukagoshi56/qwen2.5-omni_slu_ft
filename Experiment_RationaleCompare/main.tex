\begin{abstract}
Recent Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs) have emerged as a
promising approach for end-to-end Spoken Language Understanding (SLU).
While adapting SpeechLLMs to specific SLU tasks such as intent
classification requires alignment with task-specific label schemas,
conventional fine-tuning still struggles to separate semantically
adjacent intent categories.
To address this, we propose Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT),
which distills structured reasoning trajectories generated by
DeepSeek-R1, comprising candidate enumeration, rejection reasoning,
and label prediction, into SpeechLLMs as a multitask objective.
Explicitly verbalizing why incorrect candidates are rejected forces the
model to capture fine-grained distinctions and sharpen inter-class
decision boundaries.
At inference, only direct label prediction is used, incurring zero
additional computational cost.
Experiments on multiple SLU benchmarks and six SpeechLLM architectures demonstrate that RG-FT consistently improves SLU performance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Spoken language understanding (SLU) is a key component of task-oriented
dialogue systems, enabling a wide range of real-world applications such
as commercial voice
assistants~\cite{SaxonChoudharyMcKennaMouchtaris2021E2ESLUVoiceAssistants},
customer-service automation in contact
centers~\cite{WuMaoFeng2022AIOnlineCustomerService}, and supporting
front-line workers in operational
environments~\cite{LinaresGarcia2022VIVA}.
Among the core SLU tasks, this work focuses on intent classification (IC)~\cite{Atuhurra2024DomainAI} and slot filling (SF)~\cite{weld2022survey} , which jointly map an utterance to a structured semantic frame.

Recent Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs), including
SALMONN~\cite{tang2024salmonn}, Qwen2-Audio~\cite{chu2024qwen2},
Qwen2.5-Omni~\cite{xu2025qwen25omni}, and
Audio-Flamingo~\cite{goel2025audioflamingo3}, enable end-to-end SLU by
directly processing acoustic signals and generating structured textual
outputs.
Prior work on SpeechLLMs for IC and SF has actively explored
zero-shot and few-shot approaches that eliminate the need for
task-specific training~\cite{LiKeizerDoddipatla2024PromptingWhisperQASLU,
hacioglu2025speechllms, AgrawalGanapathy2025SLUUnseenICL}.
However, fine-tuning on task-specific data remains essential to achieve
competitive accuracy~\cite{li2024whisma}.
Choi et al.\ showed that fine-tuning SpeechLLMs on paired speech and
text data can recover most of the performance gap between text-only and
fully supervised settings, even with a small amount of paired
data~\cite{choi2025exploring}.
Nevertheless, correctly distinguishing semantically adjacent intents
that differ only in subtle aspects such as scope or granularity remains
a persistent challenge, as conventional fine-tuning fails to provide
sufficient separation between competing interpretations.
Acquiring such fine-grained distinctions purely from data demands a
large number of near-boundary examples, which are costly to obtain in
practice.



One promising direction for addressing this challenge is to supervise
the reasoning process that underlies fine-grained intent discrimination,
rather than relying solely on label supervision.
In text-based LLM research, rationale-based
supervision~\cite{zaidan-etal-2007-using,10.5555/1613715.1613721,lei-etal-2016-rationalizing}
and reasoning
distillation~\cite{magister-etal-2023-teaching,HoSchmidYun2023ReasoningTeachers,Fu2022ComplexityBasedPF,hsieh-etal-2023-distilling,zelikman2022starbootstrappingreasoningreasoning}
have demonstrated that injecting structured intermediate reasoning
signals into the training objective can improve downstream performance.
However, these results are largely established in text-only settings,
and it remains unclear whether such supervision transfers to
SpeechLLM-based SLU, where semantic decisions must be formed from
speech inputs exhibiting variability from speakers, prosody, and noise.

We address this gap with Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT),
a structured reasoning distillation framework for SpeechLLM-based SLU.
In this framework, structured reasoning trajectories generated by
DeepSeek-R1~\cite{Guo_2025}, consisting of candidate enumeration,
rejection reasoning, and label prediction, are distilled into
SpeechLLMs.
The core idea is that explicitly verbalizing the grounds for rejecting
incorrect candidates serves as a structural regularizer, forcing the
model to capture fine-grained distinctions and sharpen inter-class
decision boundaries.
We formulate this as a multitask learning objective, using only direct
label prediction at inference with zero additional computational cost.
We validate RG-FT on SLURP~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp} and
SpeechMassive~\cite{lee2024speechmassivemultilingualspeechdataset} across six SpeechLLM
architectures, and further provide quantitative analysis of the
resulting latent representations via Silhouette coefficients and
Fisher ratios.



Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose \textbf{RG-FT (Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning)}, a reasoning distillation framework for SpeechLLM-based SLU, in which reasoning trajectories from DeepSeek-R1 consisting of candidate enumeration, rejection reasoning, and label prediction are distilled into SpeechLLMs as an auxiliary regularizer under a multitask learning objective.
    \item Through experiments on SLURP~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp} and SpeechMassive~\cite{lee2024speechmassivemultilingualspeechdataset} across six
    SpeechLLM, we demonstrate that RG-FT consistently
    improves intent accuracy (up to $+2.79\%$) and SLU-F1 (up to
    $+1.26$ points) over standard fine-tuning, with zero additional
    computational cost at inference.
    \item We provide quantitative evidence via Silhouette coefficients, Fisher ratios, and nearest-centroid margin that reasoning supervision enhances inter-class separability of latent speech representations, corroborating the mechanism by which RG-FT addresses confusion among semantically adjacent intents.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Structured Reasoning Trajectory: $T = (C, R, L)$}
We represent the SLU decision process as a structured reasoning
trajectory $T(x) = (C, R, L)$.
Although conceptually decomposed into candidate enumeration ($C$),
rejection rationale ($R$), and final label ($L$), the trajectory is
generated as a single structured output sequence.
The trajectory $T(x) = (C, R, L)$ is operationalized through a
structured prompt, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:prompt}.

\textbf{Candidate Space ($C$):}
Given input speech $x$ and a database (DB) of all valid intent and slot definitions, the model enumerates $K$ intent candidates
and $J$ slot candidates, where each slot $s_j$ is associated with
a set of value candidates $\{v_{j,m}\}_m$:
\begin{equation}
C_I = \{i_k\}_{k=1}^{K}, \qquad
C_S = \{ s_j(\{v_{j,m}\}_m) \}_{j=1}^{J}.
\end{equation}
Here, $i_k$ denotes the $k$-th intent candidate, $s_j$ the $j$-th
slot type, and $v_{j,m}$ the $m$-th value candidate for slot $s_j$.
We assume the ground-truth intent $y^*$ is included in $C_I$.

\textbf{Rejection Rationale ($R$):}
Let $\bar{C}_I = C_I \setminus \{y^*\}$ denote the set of incorrect
intent candidates, and let $\bar{C}_S$ denote the set of incorrect
slot-type candidates. For each $c \in \bar{C}_I \cup \bar{C}_S$,
the model generates a natural language rationale $r(c; x)$ explaining
why $c$ contradicts the utterance $x$. The rejection rationale $R$ is
the collection of these paired explanations:
\begin{equation}
R = \{(c,\ r(c; x))\}_{c \in \bar{C}_I \cup \bar{C}_S}.
\end{equation}
Compared to affirmation-based reasoning, explicitly rejecting each
competing hypothesis in a contrastive manner encourages
fine-grained differentiation among semantically adjacent intents
and slot types,
thereby sharpening inter-class decision boundaries more effectively.
This design is supported by empirical evidence from contrastive
explanation generation~\cite{chen-etal-2021-kace} and Contrastive
Chain-of-Thought prompting~\cite{shim2024chainofthoughtaugmentationlogitcontrast}, as well as
theoretical arguments that counterfactual explanations promote
clearer understanding of decision boundaries~\cite{verma2021counterfactual}.

\textbf{Structured Label ($L$):}
The final structured output consists of the predicted intent $y^*$
and a set of slot--value pairs $(s_k, v_k)$, where $s_k$ is the
$k$-th predicted slot type and $v_k$ its corresponding value:
\begin{equation}
L=\{\,\texttt{intent}: y^*,\ 
\texttt{entities}: \{(s_k, v_k)\}_k\,\}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Teacher-based Data Construction}
To construct training data containing $T$, we employ
DeepSeek-R1~\cite{Guo_2025} as a teacher model.
The teacher receives the speech transcript, the ground-truth labels
$(y^*, S^*)$, and a database of all valid intent and slot definitions
to generate $T$.
Crucially, the ground-truth labels are explicitly provided to the
teacher, reducing the risk of generating rejection rationales that
contradict the correct labels.
We verified that 94.0\% of the training samples yielded well-formed
trajectories conforming to the prescribed $C \to R \to L$ format;
the remaining samples were discarded.
On average, DeepSeek-R1 enumerated 2.5 intent candidates and
1.2 slot-type candidates per utterance.

\subsection{Training Objectives}
Three training configurations are compared to isolate the contribution
of each component. Direct-FT serves as the baseline, Reasoning-FT as
an ablation, and RG-FT as the proposed method.
For a training sample $(x, T)$ where $T = (C, R, L)$, we define two
per-sample losses:
\begin{align}
  \ell_{\text{label}}(x)
    &= -\log p_\theta\!\left(L \mid x,\, g_{\text{label}}\right), \\
  \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x)
    &= -\log p_\theta\!\left(T \mid x,\, g_{\text{reasoning}}\right),
\end{align}
where $g_{\text{label}}$ and $g_{\text{reasoning}}$ are task identifiers
instructing the model to generate $L$ directly or the full trajectory
$T = (C, R, L)$, respectively.

\subsubsection{Direct Fine-Tuning (Direct-FT)}
Direct-FT maps speech $x$ directly to the structured label $L$ without
intermediate reasoning, reimplementing the protocol
of~\cite{choi2025exploring} under our exact experimental conditions for
a controlled comparison:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Direct}}
  = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
    \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}} \ell_{\text{label}}(x).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Reasoning Fine-Tuning (Reasoning-FT)}
Reasoning-FT treats the full trajectory $T$ as the sole training
objective, serving as an ablation that isolates the effect of
trajectory supervision without the complementary direct label prediction:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Reasoning}}
  = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
    \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}} \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x).
\end{equation}
At inference, the model generates $C$ and $R$ explicitly before
producing $L$, introducing both increased latency and the risk of error
propagation through the reasoning chain.

\subsubsection{Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT) --- Proposed}
Simply training on reasoning traces tends to destabilize optimization
in moderate-scale models~\cite{li-etal-2025-small-models,
chen-etal-2025-unveiling-key,chen-etal-2025-skip,hsieh-etal-2023-distilling}.
We therefore treat reasoning as an auxiliary regularizer within a
multitask framework, allowing the model to acquire representations
sensitive to fine-grained intent differences while producing labels
directly at inference without additional computational cost.
The multitask objective is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{RG-FT}}
  = \alpha
    \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_0|}
      \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}_0} \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x)
    }_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Reasoning}}}
  +\; (1-\alpha)
    \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_1|}
      \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}_1} \ell_{\text{label}}(x)
    }_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Label}}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}_0$ and $\mathcal{B}_1$ are independently sampled
mini-batches assigned to the Reasoning and Label objectives respectively.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset}
We evaluate our framework on two SLU benchmarks.
SLURP~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp} is an English dataset
comprising 11,514 training, 2,033 development, and 2,974 test
sentences covering 18 domains and 60 intent classes.
Speech-MASSIVE~\cite{lee2024speechmassivemultilingualspeechdataset}
is a multilingual speech dataset built upon the MASSIVE
corpus; we use its French subset, which provides the same
split sizes and label schema as SLURP.



\subsection{Metrics}
Performance is assessed across four complementary metrics.
Scenario and Action accuracy measure the correctness of
high-level domain classification (e.g., \textit{calendar},
\textit{email}) and specific intent action (e.g., \textit{set},
\textit{query}), respectively. Intent accuracy evaluates the
combined (Scenario, Action) pair, reflecting overall
classification correctness. SLU-F1~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp} is a metric for
entity prediction that accounts for both semantic label
matching and filler-level textual similarity.


\subsection{Latent Space Separability Analysis}
\label{sec:rep_analysis}
To quantitatively assess whether multitask reasoning supervision improves intent discriminability, we extract hidden state vectors at the moment of intent generation.
Specifically, we identify the tokens corresponding to the scenario and action fields in the generated output and pool their hidden states to form an intent representation vector per utterance.
Inter-class separability is evaluated using three complementary metrics: Silhouette coefficient~\cite{ROUSSEEUW198753}, Fisher ratio~\cite{fisher1936use}, and nearest-centroid margin, defined as
\begin{equation}
  m(z) = d(z, \mu_{y'}) - d(z, \mu_{y}),
  \label{eq:ncm}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_y$ is the centroid of the true class $y$, $y' = \arg\min_{k \neq y} d(z, \mu_k)$ is the nearest competing class, and a larger positive margin indicates clearer separation between semantically adjacent intents.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All models are fully fine-tuned using BF16 precision with the AdamW
optimizer, a learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-5}$ with cosine annealing
schedule, a batch size of 4, and 4 gradient accumulation steps.
Following \cite{choi2025exploring}, all models are trained on both
speech and gold transcription inputs to facilitate acquisition of
fundamental SLU capabilities.
The maximum number of new tokens is set to 384, and all configurations
are trained for up to 4 epochs with the best checkpoint selected based
on development set performance. All experiments are conducted on
2$\times$H200 GPUs, and results are averaged over three random seeds.

For RG-FT, we set $\alpha{=}0.5$ for all main experiments. A
sensitivity analysis over $\alpha \in \{0.1, 0.2, \ldots, 0.9\}$
confirmed that performance follows a bell-shaped trend across all six
architectures, with $\alpha{=}0.5$ consistently yielding competitive
results (see Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
\subsection{RG-FT Consistently Improves SLU Performance Across Architectures}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} shows the results of Direct-FT, 
Reasoning-FT, and RG-FT across six SpeechLLM architectures.
RG-FT consistently outperforms both baselines across all models, 
metrics, and corpora.
On SLURP, intent accuracy improves by up to $+2.79\%$ and SLU-F1 
by up to $+2.42$ points over Direct-FT, with similar gains observed 
on Speech-MASSIVE~(FR).
These results demonstrate that explicitly supervising reasoning 
as a multitask regularizer is effective for improving SLU performance 
in SpeechLLMs.
Notably, Reasoning-FT occasionally underperforms Direct-FT on both 
corpora, with intent accuracy degrading by up to $2.32\%$ 
(Qwen2.5-Omni-3B)~\cite{xu2025qwen25omni} on SLURP.
This tendency is particularly pronounced in smaller models, 
where learning resources are disproportionately allocated to 
reasoning sequence generation, leaving insufficient capacity 
for learning accurate label prediction.
This finding is consistent with prior work reporting that 
reasoning supervision alone is insufficient to stabilize 
performance in smaller 
models~\cite{li-etal-2025-small-models,chen-etal-2025-unveiling-key,chen-etal-2025-skip,hsieh-etal-2023-distilling}.

\subsection{Multitask Reasoning Sharpens Intent Clusters in Latent Space}

Table~\ref{tab:rep_analysis} reports latent space separability metrics
comparing Direct-FT and RG-FT (Qwen2.5-Omni-7B).
RG-FT improves consistently across all metrics.
The Fisher ratio rises from 1.133 to 1.276 ($+0.143$), indicating
that between-class scatter increases relative to within-class scatter,
and the mean inter-centroid cosine distance increases from 0.262 to
0.328 ($+0.067$).
The normalized nearest-centroid margin also improves from 0.174 to
0.194 ($+0.020$), confirming that decision boundaries between
semantically adjacent intents are more clearly defined under RG-FT.
Taken together, these results provide quantitative evidence that
supervising rejection reasoning as an auxiliary objective encourages
the model to internalize fine-grained inter-class distinctions,
leading to more discriminable latent representations even when
reasoning is absent at inference.

\subsection{Impact of Rationale Components}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_components} reports the contribution of
each rationale component (Qwen2.5-Omni-7B).
Adding Label~$+$~$C$ alone already yields a consistent gain over
Direct-FT ($+0.23\%$ intent accuracy, $+0.74$ SLU-F1), confirming
that enumerating competing hypotheses provides a useful inductive
signal for distinguishing semantically adjacent intents.
Label~$+$~$C{\to}R$ further improves intent accuracy ($+0.28\%$
over Label~$+$~$C$) and SLU-F1 ($+0.07$ points), demonstrating
that explicitly verbalizing why incorrect candidates are rejected
sharpens inter-class boundaries.
The full trajectory Label~$+$~$C{\to}R{\to}L$ achieves the highest
intent accuracy ($88.35\%$), while Label~$+$~$C{\to}R$ attains the
highest SLU-F1 ($77.00$).
These results validate the sequential $C \to R \to L$ design,
in which each component contributes incrementally to the
regularization effect.

\subsection{Balanced Mixing of Reasoning and Label Objectives Yields Robust Gains}
\label{sec:alpha_analysis}
Figure~\ref{fig:alpha_sensitivity} shows intent accuracy and SLU-F1
as $\alpha$ is swept from $0$ (Direct-FT) to $1$ (Reasoning-FT) across
all six architectures on the SLURP test set.
Both metrics follow a consistent bell-shaped profile across all model
families: performance improves steadily over the Direct-FT baseline
as $\alpha$ increases from $0$, peaks in the range
$\alpha \in [0.4, 0.6]$, and then degrades sharply as $\alpha$
approaches $1$.
The sharp degradation near $\alpha{=}1$ mirrors the underperformance
of Reasoning-FT observed in Table~\ref{tab:main_results}, reinforcing
the finding that reasoning supervision alone is insufficient for
moderate-scale SpeechLLMs.
Conversely, the consistent improvement over Direct-FT ($\alpha{=}0$)
across a wide range of intermediate $\alpha$ values demonstrates that
even a modest amount of reasoning supervision provides a meaningful
regularization effect on latent representations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We proposed RG-FT, a structured reasoning distillation framework
for moderate-scale SpeechLLMs, to address the challenge of
distinguishing semantically adjacent intents in spoken language
understanding. By supervising a structured reasoning process
($C \to R \to L$) alongside direct label prediction under a
multitask learning objective, we consistently improved performance
across six architectures on both SLURP and Speech-MASSIVE~(FR).
Latent space separability analysis provided quantitative evidence
that reasoning supervision encourages more separable intent clusters,
and restricting inference to direct label prediction eliminates
additional computational cost.
Several open questions remain, including quality evaluation of
distilled reasoning trajectories and how acoustic features contribute
to the reasoning process.
Future work will investigate the effect of teacher model choice,
generalization to more diverse corpora, and the impact of acoustic
conditions such as background noise on reasoning supervision.
