\begin{abstract}
Recent Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs) enable end-to-end Spoken language understanding (SLU) by directly processing acoustic inputs. However, (1) learning clear decision boundaries among competing interpretations remains challenging. In text-only LLMs, structured reasoning distillation has been shown to improve downstream performance. Yet, (2) directly training SpeechLLMs to generate reasoning traces as a solution to this problem can destabilize optimization and degrade performance.
To address both issues, we propose a structured reasoning distillation framework that integrates reasoning not as a primary generation objective but as a form of regularization. To explicitly model differences among competing interpretations, we formulate SLU as a stepwise decision process consisting of candidate enumeration, rejection of incorrect alternatives, and final label prediction. By jointly optimizing reasoning trajectories and direct label prediction in a multitask learning framework, reasoning functions as a structural regularizer that enhances boundary-sensitive representations without incurring inference-time overhead.
Experiments on the SLURP benchmark across six architectures demonstrate consistent improvements over both standard fine-tuning and reasoning-only training in intent accuracy and SLU-F1. Representation analysis further confirms improved inter-class separability in the latent space.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Spoken language understanding (SLU) is a key component of task-oriented
dialogue systems, enabling a wide range of real-world applications such
as commercial voice
assistants~\cite{SaxonChoudharyMcKennaMouchtaris2021E2ESLUVoiceAssistants},
customer-service automation in contact
centers~\cite{WuMaoFeng2022AIOnlineCustomerService}, and supporting
front-line workers in operational
environments~\cite{LinaresGarcia2022VIVA}. SLU typically comprises
intent classification~\cite{Atuhurra2024DomainAI} and slot
filling~\cite{weld2022survey} for mapping an utterance to a semantic
frame.

Recent advances in Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs), including
SALMONN~\cite{tang2024salmonn}, Qwen2-Audio~\cite{chu2024qwen2audio},
Qwen2.5-Omni~\cite{xu2025qwen25omni}, and
Audio-Flamingo~\cite{goel2025audioflamingo3}, enable end-to-end SLU by
directly processing acoustic signals and generating structured textual
outputs.

Prior work on SpeechLLMs for SLU has explored a range of approaches,
including zero-shot and prompting-based SLU that reformulates intent
detection and slot filling as question-answering
tasks~\cite{LiKeizerDoddipatla2024PromptingWhisperQASLU}, large-scale
zero-shot slot filling across diverse schema
settings~\cite{hacioglu2025speechllms,hacioglu2025slot},
parameter-efficient fine-tuning with modality
alignment~\cite{li2024whisma}, task-agnostic generalization to unseen
intents~\cite{AgrawalGanapathy2025SLUUnseenICL}, and systematic analyses
of fine-tuning strategies under limited speech
supervision~\cite{choi2025exploring}. Despite these efforts, correctly
distinguishing semantically adjacent intents that differ only in subtle
aspects such as scope or granularity remains a persistent challenge.
Learning such fine-grained distinctions purely from data requires a
substantial number of boundary examples, which are costly to collect in
practice.

Correctly identifying semantically adjacent intents requires the model
to explicitly learn fine-grained differences among candidates.
Supervision through structured intermediate reasoning offers a promising
path toward this goal. In text-based LLM research, rationale-based
supervision~\cite{zelikman2022starbootstrappingreasoningreasoning} and
reasoning
distillation~\cite{magister-etal-2023-teaching,HoSchmidYun2023ReasoningTeachers,Fu2022ComplexityBasedPF,hsieh2023distillingstepbystepoutperforminglarger}
have shown that incorporating structured intermediate reasoning can
improve downstream performance on text tasks. However, these findings
are grounded in text-only settings, and whether such supervision
transfers effectively to SpeechLLM-based SLU---where acoustic and
semantic representations must be jointly modeled---has not yet been
investigated.

In this work, we propose a structured reasoning distillation framework
for SpeechLLM-based SLU that addresses confusion among semantically
adjacent intent categories. We explicitly model SLU as a stepwise
decision process---enumerating plausible intent candidates, rejecting
incorrect alternatives with evidence-grounded justifications, and
producing a structured semantic label---and distill such structured
rationales from a large reasoning teacher into SpeechLLMs. Rather than
treating reasoning as a primary inference objective, we adopt a multitask
learning formulation that uses intermediate reasoning as a structural
regularizer for direct label prediction. By jointly optimizing reasoning
generation and direct prediction, the model acquires feature
representations that are sensitive to fine-grained differences among
intent candidates, while at inference time producing labels directly
without generating reasoning traces, incurring no additional
computational cost.

Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning(RG-FT), a structured reasoning distillation framework
    for SpeechLLM-based SLU, in which teacher-generated reasoning
    trajectories $(C \to R \to L)$ serve as an auxiliary regularizer for
    direct intent prediction under a multitask learning objective.
    \item Through experiments on SLURP across six architectures, we
    demonstrate that RG-FT consistently improves intent accuracy and
    SLU-F1 over standard fine-tuning, with intent accuracy gains of up
    to $+2.79\%$ and SLU-F1 improvements of up to $+1.26$ points.
    \item We provide quantitative evidence via Silhouette coefficients,
    Fisher ratio, and nearest-centroid margin that multitask reasoning
    supervision improves inter-class separability in the latent
    representation space.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed Method}


\subsection{Structured Reasoning Trajectory: $T = (C, R, L)$}

We represent the SLU decision process as a structured reasoning
trajectory $T(x) = (C, R, L)$.
Although conceptually decomposed into candidate enumeration ($C$),
rejection rationale ($R$), and final label ($L$), the trajectory is
generated as a single structured output sequence.

\textbf{Candidate Space ($C$):}
Given input speech $x$, the model enumerates intent and slot
candidates:
\begin{equation}
C_I = \{i_k\}_{k=1}^{K}, \qquad
C_S = \{ s_j(\{v_{j,m}\}_m) \}_{j=1}^{J}.
\end{equation}
We assume the ground-truth intent $y^*$ is included in $C_I$.

\textbf{Rejection Rationale ($R$):}
Let $\bar{C}_I = C_I \setminus \{y^*\}$ denote incorrect intent
candidates. For each $c \in \bar{C}_I$, the model produces a
contradiction evidence string $r(c; x)$ grounded in the utterance.
The rejection rationale is defined as
\begin{equation}
R = \bigoplus_{c \in \bar{C}_I} (c \Vert r(c; x)),
\end{equation}
where $\bigoplus$ denotes concatenation.
Unlike affirmation-based reasoning, which merely describes
properties of the correct intent, rejection explicitly requires
the model to articulate why each competing hypothesis contradicts
the utterance. This contrastive elimination forces the model to
identify fine-grained distinctions among semantically adjacent
intents, thereby sharpening inter-class decision boundaries.

\textbf{Structured Label ($L$):}
The final structured output is
\begin{equation}
L=\{\,\texttt{intent}: y^*,\ 
\texttt{entities}: \{(s_k, v_k)\}_k\,\}.
\end{equation}


\subsection{Teacher-based Data Construction}

To construct training data containing $T$, we employ DeepSeek-R1 as a
teacher model. The teacher receives the speech transcript, the
ground-truth labels $(y^*, S^*)$, and a database of all valid intent and
slot definitions. It is instructed to generate, for each $c \in \bar{C}$,
a specific contradiction with the utterance content
(Fig.~\ref{fig:prompt}). This oracle-guided construction is intended to
improve the logical consistency of the reasoning trajectories used for
distillation, by constraining the teacher to generate rationales that do
not contradict the ground-truth labels. Note that the teacher operates
on text transcripts rather than raw audio, leveraging its strong language
reasoning capability; the student model is then trained to align its
acoustic representations with the resulting structured rationales.


\subsection{Proposed Method: Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT)}

Simply training on reasoning traces as the primary generation objective
tends to destabilize optimization in moderate-scale models, sometimes
degrading
performance~\cite{li-etal-2025-small-models,chen-etal-2025-unveiling-key,
chen-etal-2025-skip,hsieh-etal-2023-distilling}. To avoid this, we
instead treat reasoning as a regularizer for direct label prediction
within a multitask learning framework. By jointly optimizing reasoning
generation as an auxiliary task, the model acquires feature
representations that are sensitive to fine-grained differences among
intent candidates, while at inference time producing labels directly
without generating reasoning traces, incurring no additional
computational cost.

Each training sample $(x_i, T_i, L_i)$ is expanded into two optimization
targets distinguished by a task identifier:
\begin{align}
  \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x_i)
    &= -\log p_\theta\!\left(T_i \mid x_i,\, g_{\text{reasoning}}\right) \\
  \ell_{\text{label}}(x_i)
    &= -\log p_\theta\!\left(L_i \mid x_i,\, g_{\text{label}}\right)
\end{align}
where $g_{\text{reasoning}}$ instructs the model to generate the full
trajectory $T = (C, R, L)$, and $g_{\text{label}}$ instructs it to
generate the structured label $L$ directly. The multitask objective is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{RG-FT}}
  = \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_0|}
      \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_0} \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x_i)
    }_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Reasoning}}}
  +\; \alpha
    \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_1|}
      \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_1} \ell_{\text{label}}(x_i)
    }_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Label}}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}_0$ and $\mathcal{B}_1$ are the subsets of a
mini-batch assigned to the Reasoning and Label branches respectively,
and $\alpha{=}0.5$ throughout all experiments. Because each training
sample generates one item per branch, the two branches receive balanced
supervision within every batch.


\subsection{Baseline: Direct Fine-Tuning (Direct-FT)}

Direct-FT maps speech $x$ to the structured label $L$ without
intermediate reasoning, reimplementing the protocol
of~\cite{choi2025exploring} under our exact experimental conditions for
a controlled comparison:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Direct}}
  = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
    \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}} \ell_{\text{label}}(x_i)
\end{equation}


\subsection{Alternative: Reasoning Fine-Tuning (Reasoning-FT)}

Reasoning-FT treats the full trajectory $T$ as the sole training
objective, serving as an ablation that isolates the effect of
reasoning-only supervision without the complementary Label branch:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Reasoning}}
  = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
    \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}} \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x_i)
\end{equation}
At inference, the model generates $C$ and $R$ explicitly before
producing $L$, introducing both increased latency and the risk of error
propagation through the reasoning chain.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset}
We evaluate our framework on the SLURP
dataset~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp}, which comprises
11,514 training, 2,033 development, and 2,974 test utterances
covering 18 domains and 60 intent classes. Following
\cite{choi2025exploring}, all models are trained on both speech
and gold transcription inputs to facilitate acquisition of
fundamental SLU capabilities.

\subsection{Metrics}
Performance is assessed across four complementary metrics.
Scenario and Action accuracy measure the correctness of
high-level domain classification (e.g., \textit{calendar},
\textit{email}) and specific intent action (e.g., \textit{set},
\textit{query}), respectively. Intent accuracy evaluates the
combined (Scenario, Action) pair, reflecting overall
classification correctness. SLU-F1~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp}
provides a holistic measure of the full semantic frame,
penalizing slot errors more heavily when the intent is incorrect.


\subsection{Representation Analysis}
\label{sec:rep_analysis}
To quantitatively assess whether multitask reasoning supervision
improves intent discriminability, we extract hidden state vectors
at the moment of intent generation. Specifically, we identify the
tokens corresponding to the scenario and action values in the
generated output and pool their hidden states to obtain a single
intent representation vector per utterance. We evaluate
inter-class separability using three complementary metrics.

The \textbf{Silhouette coefficient}~\cite{rousseeuw1987silhouettes}
measures, for each sample, how much closer it is to its own class
than to the nearest competing class using cosine distance. The
overall score approaches $1$ when clusters are well-separated.

The \textbf{Fisher ratio}~\cite{fisher1936use} measures the ratio
of between-class scatter to within-class scatter in the
representation space. A higher value indicates that intent classes
are more discriminable.

The \textbf{nearest-centroid margin} measures, for each sample,
the difference between its distance to the nearest incorrect class
centroid and its distance to the correct class centroid. A larger
positive margin indicates a clearer decision boundary between
semantically adjacent intents.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All models are fine-tuned in BF16 precision using AdamW with a learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-5}$,
a batch size of 4, and 4 gradient accumulation steps. Training is conducted on two NVIDIA H200 GPUs.
Direct-FT and Reasoning-FT are trained for 2 epochs.
For RG-FT, each training sample provides supervision to both the Reasoning and Label branches within a mini-batch.
To keep the total number of optimization (parameter update) steps comparable across training conditions,
we train RG-FT for 1 epoch.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Results}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports Scenario, Action, Intent
accuracy and SLU-F1 for three training conditions across six
architectures: Direct-FT, Reasoning-FT, and the proposed RG-FT
using Label-branch ($g_{\text{label}}$) inference.
RG-FT consistently outperforms both baselines across all models
and metrics, with intent accuracy gains ranging from
$+0.96\%$ (Qwen2.5-Omni-7B) to $+2.79\%$ (Qwen2.5-Omni-3B)
over Direct-FT, and SLU-F1 improvements of up to $+1.26$ points
(Qwen2.5-Omni-7B).
Notably, Reasoning-FT occasionally underperforms Direct-FT, with
intent accuracy degrading by up to $2.32\%$ (Qwen2.5-Omni-3B),
indicating that reasoning supervision without a complementary
Label-branch objective does not reliably improve performance in
moderate-scale models.
These results demonstrate that explicitly supervising reasoning
as a multitask regularizer is more effective than treating it as
a primary generation objective for moderate-scale SpeechLLMs.


\subsection{Representation Analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:rep_analysis} reports representation geometry
metrics comparing Direct-FT and RG-FT (Qwen2.5-Omni-7B).
RG-FT improves across all separability metrics: the Silhouette
coefficient (cosine) increases from 0.242 to 0.279 ($+0.037$),
and the Fisher ratio rises from 1.133 to 1.276 ($+0.143$),
indicating greater between-class scatter relative to within-class
scatter. The mean inter-centroid cosine distance also increases
from 0.262 to 0.328 ($+0.067$), suggesting that intent clusters
are more evenly spread across the representation space.
Furthermore, the normalized nearest-centroid margin improves from
0.174 to 0.194 ($+0.020$), confirming that decision boundaries
between semantically adjacent intents become more clearly defined
under RG-FT.

\subsection{Impact of Rationale Components}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_components} reports intent accuracy and
SLU-F1 for each rationale component configuration using
Label-branch ($g_{\text{label}}$) inference. Among individual
components, $C$ yields the largest improvement in intent accuracy
($+0.37\%$ over Direct-FT), indicating that explicitly enumerating
candidate intents contributes most to sharpening decision
boundaries. $R$ alone provides marginal gains, suggesting that
rejection rationale is insufficient as a standalone supervisory
signal. The full configuration $C+R+L$ achieves the highest intent
accuracy, while $C+L$ and $C+R+L$ both attain the highest SLU-F1
of 77.18, indicating that the combination of candidate enumeration
and rejection rationale contributes to stable performance across
metrics.



The results indicate that providing Intent Candidates ($C$) yields
the most significant initial boost in intent accuracy ($+0.37\%$
over Direct-FT). This supports our hypothesis that forcing the
model to explicitly contrast similar intent categories early in
the process sharpens the decision boundaries. While Rejection
Rationale ($R$) alone provides marginal gains, its combination
with candidates in $C+R+L$ achieves the highest overall accuracy
and stability across domains.


\subsection{Inference Branch: Reasoning-branch vs.\ Label-branch}
Table~\ref{tab:branch_comparison} reports intent accuracy and
SLU-F1 for each multitask configuration under two inference
modes: Reasoning-branch ($g_{\text{reasoning}}$) and Label-branch
($g_{\text{label}}$). Across all configurations, Label-branch
consistently achieves higher performance than Reasoning-branch.
Notably, the performance gap is particularly pronounced in the
RG-FT w/ R+L configuration, where Label-branch outperforms
Reasoning-branch by $2.06\%$ in intent accuracy. This trend
suggests that Reasoning-branch supervision effectively shapes the
shared representation space during training, but the benefit does
not transfer to inference-time rationale generation. Rather, the
structured reasoning internalized through Reasoning-branch
training is more effectively utilized when the model performs
direct prediction via Label-branch, without the overhead of
generating intermediate rationale sequences.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
In this paper, we proposed RG-FT, a structured reasoning distillation
framework for moderate-scale SpeechLLMs, to address the challenge of
distinguishing semantically adjacent intents in spoken language
understanding. By supervising a structured reasoning process
($C \to R \to L$) alongside direct label prediction under a multitask
learning objective, we consistently improved performance across six
distinct model architectures on the SLURP benchmark. Representation
analysis via Silhouette coefficients, Fisher ratio, and nearest-centroid
margin provided quantitative evidence that multitask reasoning supervision
encourages the model to develop more separable intent clusters in the
latent space. Crucially, we demonstrated that treating reasoning as a
training regularizer while performing direct inference via Label-branch
allows the model to leverage the benefits of structured reasoning without
the overhead or noise of inference-time rationale generation. Future work
will explore the scalability of this approach to smaller edge-device
models and its application to multilingual SLU scenarios.
