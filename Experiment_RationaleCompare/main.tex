\begin{abstract}
Recent Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs) have emerged as a
promising approach for end-to-end Spoken Language Understanding (SLU).
While adapting SpeechLLMs to specific SLU tasks such as intent
classification requires alignment with task-specific label schemas,
conventional fine-tuning still struggles to separate semantically
adjacent intent categories.
To address this, we propose Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT),
which distills structured reasoning trajectories generated by
DeepSeek-R1, comprising candidate enumeration, rejection reasoning,
and label prediction, into SpeechLLMs as a multitask objective.
Explicitly verbalizing why incorrect candidates are rejected forces the
model to capture fine-grained distinctions and sharpen inter-class
decision boundaries.
At inference, only direct label prediction is used, incurring zero
additional computational cost.
Experiments on an SLU benchmark across six architectures demonstrate
that RG-FT consistently improves SLU performance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Spoken language understanding (SLU) is a key component of task-oriented
dialogue systems, enabling a wide range of real-world applications such
as commercial voice
assistants~\cite{SaxonChoudharyMcKennaMouchtaris2021E2ESLUVoiceAssistants},
customer-service automation in contact
centers~\cite{WuMaoFeng2022AIOnlineCustomerService}, and supporting
front-line workers in operational
environments~\cite{LinaresGarcia2022VIVA}.
Among the core SLU tasks, intent
classification (IC)~\cite{Atuhurra2024DomainAI} and slot
filling (SF)~\cite{weld2022survey} are of particular relevance to this work,
as they jointly map an utterance to a structured semantic frame; the
remainder of this paper focuses on these two tasks.

Recent advances in Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs), including
SALMONN~\cite{tang2024salmonn}, Qwen2-Audio~\cite{chu2024qwen2audio},
Qwen2.5-Omni~\cite{xu2025qwen25omni}, and
Audio-Flamingo~\cite{goel2025audioflamingo3}, enable end-to-end SLU by
directly processing acoustic signals and generating structured textual
outputs.
Prior work on SpeechLLMs for IC and SF has explored a range of
approaches.
\cite{LiKeizerDoddipatla2024PromptingWhisperQASLU} reformulated intent
detection and slot filling as question-answering tasks, demonstrating
that SpeechLLMs can perform SLU in a zero-shot prompting regime without
task-specific training.
\cite{hacioglu2025speechllms} tackled large-scale zero-shot slot
filling, revealing systematic performance and robustness gaps and showing
that improvements to training data, architecture, and strategy can
substantially narrow them.
\cite{AgrawalGanapathy2025SLUUnseenICL} demonstrated that
task-agnostic fine-tuning with randomized class labels unlocks few-shot
generalization to entirely unseen intents without task-specific
annotations.
While such zero-shot and few-shot approaches offer flexibility,
fine-tuning on task-specific data remains necessary to achieve
competitive accuracy.
\cite{li2024whisma} showed that coupling a modality aligner with
parameter-efficient LoRA fine-tuning enables strong performance across
diverse SLU benchmarks, and \cite{choi2025exploring} established that
even a small fraction of paired speech data suffices to recover most of
the gap between text-only and fully supervised fine-tuning.
Despite these efforts, correctly distinguishing semantically adjacent
intents that differ only in subtle aspects such as scope or granularity
remains a persistent challenge, as conventional fine-tuning approaches
fail to provide sufficient separation between competing interpretations.
Acquiring such fine-grained distinctions purely from data demands a
large number of near-boundary examples, which are costly to obtain in
practice.
Figure~\ref{fig:proposed_method} provides an overview of the proposed
framework. The following subsections describe each component in detail.

  \label{fig:proposed_method}
\end{figure}

One promising direction for addressing this challenge is to supervise
the reasoning process that underlies fine-grained intent discrimination,
rather than relying solely on label supervision.
In text-based LLM research, rationale-based supervision~\cite{zaidan-etal-2007-using,10.5555/1613715.1613721,lei-etal-2016-rationalizing} and reasoning distillation~\cite{magister-etal-2023-teaching,HoSchmidYun2023ReasoningTeachers,Fu2022ComplexityBasedPF,hsieh2023distillingstepbystepoutperforminglarger,zelikman2022starbootstrappingreasoningreasoning} have demonstrated that injecting structured intermediate reasoning signals into the training objective can improve downstream performance.
However, these results are largely established in text-only settings.
It remains unclear whether such supervision transfers to SpeechLLM-based SLU, where semantic decisions must be formed from acoustically entangled representations that exhibit variability from speakers, prosody, and noise.
This acoustic--semantic coupling makes fine-grained intent discrimination particularly challenging under limited supervision, especially for semantically adjacent intent categories.

We address this gap with Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT),
a structured reasoning distillation framework for SpeechLLM-based SLU.
In this framework, structured reasoning trajectories generated by
DeepSeek-R1~\cite{Guo_2025}---each consisting of candidate
enumeration, rejection reasoning, and label prediction---are distilled
into SpeechLLMs.
The core idea is that explicitly verbalizing the grounds for rejecting
incorrect candidates serves as a structural regularizer, forcing the
model to capture fine-grained distinctions and sharpen inter-class
decision boundaries.
We formulate this as a multitask learning objective, using only direct
label prediction at inference with zero additional computational cost.
We validate RG-FT on SLURP~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp} across
six SpeechLLM architectures, and further provide quantitative analysis
of the resulting latent representations via Silhouette coefficients and
Fisher ratios.



Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose \textbf{RG-FT (Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning)}, a
    structured reasoning distillation framework for SpeechLLM-based SLU,
    in which reasoning trajectories generated by
    DeepSeek-R1---consisting of candidate enumeration, rejection
    reasoning, and label prediction $(C \to R \to L)$---are distilled
    into SpeechLLMs as an auxiliary regularizer for direct intent
    prediction under a multitask learning objective.
    \item Through experiments on SLURP across six SpeechLLM
    architectures, we demonstrate that RG-FT consistently improves
    intent accuracy (up to $+2.79\%$) and SLU-F1 (up to $+1.26$
    points) over standard fine-tuning, with zero additional
    computational cost at inference.
    \item We provide quantitative evidence via Silhouette coefficients,
    Fisher ratios, and nearest-centroid margin that multitask reasoning
    supervision enhances inter-class separability of latent speech
    representations, corroborating the mechanism by which RG-FT
    addresses confusion among semantically adjacent intent categories.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Structured Reasoning Trajectory: $T = (C, R, L)$}
We represent the SLU decision process as a structured reasoning
trajectory $T(x) = (C, R, L)$.
Although conceptually decomposed into candidate enumeration ($C$),
rejection rationale ($R$), and final label ($L$), the trajectory is
generated as a single structured output sequence.
The trajectory $T(x) = (C, R, L)$ is operationalized through a
structured prompt, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:prompt}.

\textbf{Candidate Space ($C$):}
Given input speech $x$, the model enumerates $K$ intent candidates
and $J$ slot candidates, where each slot $s_j$ is associated with
a set of value candidates $\{v_{j,m}\}_m$:
\begin{equation}
C_I = \{i_k\}_{k=1}^{K}, \qquad
C_S = \{ s_j(\{v_{j,m}\}_m) \}_{j=1}^{J}.
\end{equation}
Here, $i_k$ denotes the $k$-th intent candidate, $s_j$ the $j$-th
slot type, and $v_{j,m}$ the $m$-th value candidate for slot $s_j$.
We assume the ground-truth intent $y^*$ is included in $C_I$.

\textbf{Rejection Rationale ($R$):}
Let $\bar{C}_I = C_I \setminus \{y^*\}$ denote the set of incorrect
intent candidates. For each $c \in \bar{C}_I$, the model generates
a natural language rationale $r(c; x)$ explaining why $c$
contradicts the utterance $x$. The rejection rationale $R$ is
the collection of these paired explanations:
\begin{equation}
R = \{(c,\ r(c; x))\}_{c \in \bar{C}_I}.
\end{equation}
Compared to affirmation-based reasoning, explicitly rejecting each
competing hypothesis in a contrastive manner encourages
fine-grained differentiation among semantically adjacent intents,
thereby sharpening inter-class decision boundaries more effectively.
This design is supported by empirical evidence from contrastive
explanation generation~\cite{chen-etal-2021-kace} and Contrastive
Chain-of-Thought prompting~\cite{shim2024chainofthoughtaugmentationlogitcontrast}, as well as
theoretical arguments that counterfactual explanations promote
clearer understanding of decision boundaries~\cite{verma2021counterfactual}.

\textbf{Structured Label ($L$):}
The final structured output consists of the predicted intent $y^*$
and a set of slot--value pairs $(s_k, v_k)$, where $s_k$ is the
$k$-th predicted slot type and $v_k$ its corresponding value:
\begin{equation}
L=\{\,\texttt{intent}: y^*,\ 
\texttt{entities}: \{(s_k, v_k)\}_k\,\}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Teacher-based Data Construction}

To construct training data containing $T$, we employ DeepSeek-R1 as a
teacher model. The teacher receives the speech transcript, the
ground-truth labels $(y^*, S^*)$, and a database of all valid intent and
slot definitions. It is instructed to generate, for each $c \in \bar{C}$,
a specific contradiction with the utterance content
(Fig.~\ref{fig:proposed_method}). This oracle-guided construction is intended to
improve the logical consistency of the reasoning trajectories used for
distillation, by constraining the teacher to generate rationales that do
not contradict the ground-truth labels. Note that the teacher operates
on text transcripts rather than raw audio, leveraging its strong language
reasoning capability; the student model is then trained to align its
acoustic representations with the resulting structured rationales.

\subsection{Training Objectives}

Three training configurations are compared to isolate the contribution
of each component. Direct-FT serves as the baseline, Reasoning-FT as
an ablation, and RG-FT as the proposed method.

\subsubsection{Direct Fine-Tuning (Direct-FT)}

Direct-FT maps speech $x$ directly to the structured label $L$ without
intermediate reasoning, reimplementing the protocol
of~\cite{choi2025exploring} under our exact experimental conditions for
a controlled comparison:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Direct}}
  = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
    \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}} \ell_{\text{label}}(x).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Reasoning Fine-Tuning (Reasoning-FT)}

Reasoning-FT treats the full trajectory $T$ as the sole training
objective, serving as an ablation that isolates the effect of
reasoning-only supervision without the complementary Label branch:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Reasoning}}
  = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
    \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}} \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x).
\end{equation}
At inference, the model generates $C$ and $R$ explicitly before
producing $L$, introducing both increased latency and the risk of error
propagation through the reasoning chain.


\subsubsection{Reasoning-Guided Fine-Tuning (RG-FT) --- Proposed}

Simply training on reasoning traces tends to destabilize optimization
in moderate-scale models~\cite{li-etal-2025-small-models,
chen-etal-2025-unveiling-key,chen-etal-2025-skip,hsieh-etal-2023-distilling}.
We therefore treat reasoning as an auxiliary regularizer within a
multitask framework, allowing the model to acquire representations
sensitive to fine-grained intent differences while producing labels
directly at inference without additional computational cost.

Each training sample $(x, T, L)$ is expanded into two optimization
targets distinguished by a task identifier:
\begin{align}
  \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x)
    &= -\log p_\theta\!\left(T \mid x,\, g_{\text{reasoning}}\right), \\
  \ell_{\text{label}}(x)
    &= -\log p_\theta\!\left(L \mid x,\, g_{\text{label}}\right),
\end{align}
where $g_{\text{reasoning}}$ instructs the model to generate the full
trajectory $T = (C, R, L)$, and $g_{\text{label}}$ instructs it to
generate the structured label $L$ directly. The multitask objective is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{RG-FT}}
  = \alpha
    \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_0|}
      \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}_0} \ell_{\text{reasoning}}(x)
    }_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Reasoning}}}
  +\; (1-\alpha)
    \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_1|}
      \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}_1} \ell_{\text{label}}(x)
    }_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Label}}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}_0$ and $\mathcal{B}_1$ are independently sampled
mini-batches assigned to the Reasoning and Label branches respectively,
and $\alpha{=}0.5$ throughout all experiments, yielding equal weight
to both branches.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset}
We evaluate our framework on the SLURP
dataset~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp}, which comprises
11,514 training, 2,033 development, and 2,974 test utterances
covering 18 domains and 46 action classes. Following
\cite{choi2025exploring}, all models are trained on both speech
and gold transcription inputs to facilitate acquisition of
fundamental SLU capabilities.

\subsection{Metrics}
Performance is assessed across four complementary metrics.
Scenario and Action accuracy measure the correctness of
high-level domain classification (e.g., \textit{calendar},
\textit{email}) and specific intent action (e.g., \textit{set},
\textit{query}), respectively. Intent accuracy evaluates the
combined (Scenario, Action) pair, reflecting overall
classification correctness. SLU-F1~\cite{bastianelli-etal-2020-slurp}
provides a holistic measure of the full semantic frame,
penalizing slot errors more heavily when the intent is incorrect.


\subsection{Representation Analysis}
\label{sec:rep_analysis}
To quantitatively assess whether multitask reasoning supervision
improves intent discriminability, we extract hidden state vectors
at the moment of intent generation. Specifically, we identify the
tokens corresponding to the scenario and action values in the
generated output and pool their hidden states to obtain a single
intent representation vector per utterance. We evaluate
inter-class separability using three complementary metrics.

The \textbf{Silhouette coefficient}~\cite{rousseeuw1987silhouettes}
measures, for each sample, how much closer it is to its own class
than to the nearest competing class using cosine distance. The
overall score approaches $1$ when clusters are well-separated.

The \textbf{Fisher ratio}~\cite{fisher1936use} measures the ratio
of between-class scatter to within-class scatter in the
representation space. A higher value indicates that intent classes
are more discriminable.

The \textbf{nearest-centroid margin} measures, for each sample,
the difference between its distance to the nearest incorrect class
centroid and its distance to the correct class centroid. A larger
positive margin indicates a clearer decision boundary between
semantically adjacent intents.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All models are fine-tuned using BF16 precision with the AdamW
optimizer, a learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-5}$, a batch size
of 4, and 4 gradient accumulation steps. Oracle rationales for
multitask training are generated via DeepSeek-R1, whose
chain-of-thought pretraining makes it well-suited for producing
structured, evidence-grounded reasoning traces in the
$C \to R \to L$ format. For Direct-FT and Reasoning-FT, training proceeds for 4 epochs. 
For RG-FT, each training step simultaneously processes samples for 
both the Reasoning and Label branches, doubling the effective 
data exposure per update compared to the single-task baselines. 
To ensure a fair comparison in terms of the total,
we train RG-FT for 2 epochs.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Results}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports Scenario, Action, Intent
accuracy and SLU-F1 for three training conditions across six
architectures: Direct-FT, Reasoning-FT, and the proposed RG-FT
using Label-branch ($g_{\text{label}}$) inference.
RG-FT consistently outperforms both baselines across all models
and metrics, with intent accuracy gains ranging from
$+0.96\%$ (Qwen2.5-Omni-7B) to $+2.79\%$ (Qwen2.5-Omni-3B)
over Direct-FT, and SLU-F1 improvements of up to $+1.26$ points
(Qwen2.5-Omni-7B).
Notably, Reasoning-FT occasionally underperforms Direct-FT, with
intent accuracy degrading by up to $2.32\%$ (Qwen2.5-Omni-3B),
indicating that reasoning supervision without a complementary
Label-branch objective does not reliably improve performance in
moderate-scale models.
These results demonstrate that explicitly supervising reasoning
as a multitask regularizer is more effective than treating it as
a primary generation objective for moderate-scale SpeechLLMs.


\subsection{Representation Analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:rep_analysis} reports representation geometry
metrics comparing Direct-FT and RG-FT (Qwen2.5-Omni-7B).
RG-FT improves across all separability metrics: the Silhouette
coefficient (cosine) increases from 0.242 to 0.279 ($+0.037$),
and the Fisher ratio rises from 1.133 to 1.276 ($+0.143$),
indicating greater between-class scatter relative to within-class
scatter. The mean inter-centroid cosine distance also increases
from 0.262 to 0.328 ($+0.067$), suggesting that intent clusters
are more evenly spread across the representation space.
Furthermore, the normalized nearest-centroid margin improves from
0.174 to 0.194 ($+0.020$), confirming that decision boundaries
between semantically adjacent intents become more clearly defined
under RG-FT.

\subsection{Impact of Rationale Components}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_components} reports intent accuracy and
SLU-F1 for each rationale component configuration using
Label-branch ($g_{\text{label}}$) inference (Qwen2-Audio-7B-Instruct).
Among individual components, $C$ yields a notable improvement in
intent accuracy ($+0.51\%$ over Direct-FT), indicating that
explicitly enumerating candidate intents provides a strong
inductive signal for distinguishing semantically adjacent intents.
Interestingly, $R$ alone achieves comparable intent accuracy
($+0.84\%$) but substantially lower SLU-F1 ($70.78$, nearly
identical to Direct-FT at $70.83$), suggesting that rejection
rationale without candidate context improves intent boundary
sharpness in isolation, yet fails to propagate this benefit to
slot-level prediction. This asymmetry indicates that $R$ is not a
standalone supervisory signal but rather depends on the structured
context established by $C$ to function effectively---a finding
consistent with the sequential design of the proposed
$C \to R \to L$ trajectory, in which candidate enumeration
provides the necessary referential grounding for subsequent
rejection reasoning.

The combination $C+R$ further improves both intent accuracy
($+1.24\%$) and SLU-F1 ($+1.97$ points) over Direct-FT,
confirming that the two components are mutually reinforcing rather
than independently sufficient. The full configuration $C+R+L$
achieves the highest intent accuracy ($86.38\%$), while $C+R$
attains the highest SLU-F1 ($72.80$), with $C+R+L$ performing
comparably ($72.79$). Taken together, these results validate the
integrated design of the $C \to R \to L$ trajectory: each
component contributes incrementally, and the structured ordering
is essential to the effectiveness of rejection reasoning as a
regularization signal.


\subsection{Inference Branch: Reasoning-branch vs.\ Label-branch}
Table~\ref{tab:branch_comparison} reports intent accuracy and
SLU-F1 for each multitask configuration under two inference
modes: Reasoning-branch ($g_{\text{reasoning}}$) and Label-branch
($g_{\text{label}}$). Across all configurations, Label-branch
consistently achieves higher performance than Reasoning-branch.
Notably, the performance gap is particularly pronounced in the
RG-FT w/ R+L configuration, where Label-branch outperforms
Reasoning-branch by $2.06\%$ in intent accuracy. This trend
suggests that Reasoning-branch supervision effectively shapes the
shared representation space during training, but the benefit does
not transfer to inference-time rationale generation. Rather, the
structured reasoning internalized through Reasoning-branch
training is more effectively utilized when the model performs
direct prediction via Label-branch, without the overhead of
generating intermediate rationale sequences.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
In this paper, we proposed RG-FT, a structured reasoning distillation
framework for moderate-scale SpeechLLMs, to address the challenge of
distinguishing semantically adjacent intents in spoken language
understanding. By supervising a structured reasoning process
($C \to R \to L$) alongside direct label prediction under a multitask
learning objective, we consistently improved performance across six
distinct model architectures on the SLURP benchmark. Representation
analysis via Silhouette coefficients, Fisher ratio, and nearest-centroid
margin provided quantitative evidence that multitask reasoning supervision
encourages the model to develop more separable intent clusters in the
latent space. Crucially, we demonstrated that treating reasoning as a
training regularizer while performing direct inference via Label-branch
allows the model to leverage the benefits of structured reasoning without
the overhead or noise of inference-time rationale generation. Future work
will explore the scalability of this approach to smaller edge-device
models and its application to multilingual SLU scenarios.
